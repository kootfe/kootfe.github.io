Networking: IP, NAT, and Neighborhoods

Jan 28, 2026 | Koofte

Hello there. Today, we are diving into the world of Networking. A friend of mine is getting into DevOps and asked for help, so I’m making this series for him—and for you! If you hate DevOps... well, you can keep hating it. I do too. LOL. But you still gotta learn it!

In this series, we’re covering everything from IP addresses to WebSockets and custom protocols. Let’s roll!

IP and Ports: The Apartment Analogy

When people talk about networking, two things usually come up: IP and Port. (And MAC addresses if you’re a weirdo, but let’s skip that for now).

Think of an IP Address like a building's street address. If I send data to 

1.1.1.1
, that’s your house!

But wait... because of how the modern world works, we aren’t living in single-family houses. We’re living in apartments. If the IP is the building address, the Port is the apartment number. Every transport protocol - yeah there is protocols of internet that does not carry data but do other things - has 65,536 available ports. So, if I send data to 

1.1.1.1:8080
, I’m sending a package to Building 1.1.1.1, Apartment 8080. Simple, right?

IPv4 vs. IPv6

Wait, I said 

1.1.1.1
, but you might see 
abcd:abcd:abcd:abcd:abcd:abcd:abcd:abcd
... or just 
abcd::
. Congrats, you’ve found IPv6.

Why does it exist? Not because IPv4 sucks, but because we ran out of them. However, most DevOps and IT people hate IPv6. A wise IT guy once said: "If your client wants you to set up IPv6 and TLS, just cut the Ethernet cable to enable the firewall."

Neighborhoods: WAN vs. LAN

  • WAN (Wide Area Network): This is the Internet. The big, scary outside world. Google, YouTube, and that weird forum you visit at 3 AM? That’s all WAN.
  • LAN (Local Area Network): This is your private 'inner circle.' It’s the network inside your house managed by your router.

NAT: The Gatekeeper

IPv4 addresses are running out. NAT is the 'gatekeeper' that lets an entire household share one single Public IP.

  1. Loopback (
    127.0.0.0
    - 
    127.255.255.255
    ):     This is 'Home.' Data sent here never leaves your computer.
  2. Private IPs (
    192.168.x.x
    ):     These are the addresses your router gives out. Your phone might be 
    192.168.1.5
    and your PC 
    192.168.1.10
    .
