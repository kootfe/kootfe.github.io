Networking: IP, NAT, and Neighborhoods

Jan 28, 2026 | Koofte

Hello there.

Today, we are diving into the world of Networking. A friend of mine is getting into DevOps and asked for help, so I’m making this series for him—and for you! If you hate DevOps... well, you can keep hating it. I do too. LOL. But you still gotta learn it!

In this series, we’re covering everything from IP addresses to WebSockets and custom protocols. Let’s roll!

When people talk about networking, two things usually come up: IP and Port. (And MAC addresses if you’re a weirdo, but let’s skip that for now).

Think of an IP Address like a building's street address. If I send data to 1.1.1.1, that’s your house!

But wait... because of how the modern world works, we aren’t living in single-family houses. We’re living in apartments. If the IP is the building address, the Port is the apartment number. Every transport protocol—yeah there are protocols of the internet that don’t carry data but do other things—has 65,536 available ports. So, if I send data to 1.1.1.1:8080, I’m sending a package to Building 1.1.1.1, Apartment 8080. Simple, right?

Wait, I said 1.1.1.1, but you might see abcd:abcd:abcd:abcd:abcd:abcd:abcd:abcd... or just abcd::. Congrats, you’ve found IPv6.

Why does it exist? Not because IPv4 sucks, but because we ran out of them. However, most DevOps and IT people hate IPv6. A wise IT guy once said: "If your client wants you to set up IPv6 and TLS, just cut the Ethernet cable to enable the firewall."

It works perfectly fine with TLS—it genuinely doesn't care—but it’s a total pain in the ass to debug. IPv4 is exhausted; we literally cannot give new IPv4 addresses to everyone. That’s why things like NAT exist. IPv6 is massive and designed to work without NAT. Using NAT with IPv6 is like inventing a car to go faster but hitching a horse to the front of it. It kills the purpose!

Neighborhoods: WAN vs. LAN

  • WAN (Wide Area Network): The Internet. Google, YouTube, and that weird forum you visit at 3 AM? That’s all WAN.
  • LAN (Local Area Network): Your private 'inner circle.' The network inside your house managed by your router. You don’t even need the internet to have a LAN; two computers can talk via a cable.

Loopback

Loopback (127.0.0.1): This is 'Home.' Data sent here never leaves your computer. It’s like talking to yourself in the mirror—you shout, you hear it, nobody else can. (Fun fact: the whole 127.x.x.x range, more than 16 million IPs, is reserved for this.)

NAT: The Gatekeeper

IPv4 addresses are running out. NAT is the 'gatekeeper' that lets an entire household share one single Public IP.

Private IPs (192.168.x.x): These are the addresses your router gives out. Your phone might be 192.168.1.5 and your PC 192.168.1.10. These are invisible to the outside world. More like… it never even leaves their router if they send a request. But that’s up to them, not you.

The Middleman: When your PC wants to visit Google, it sends a request to your router. The router goes, “Okay, I’ll go get that for you.” It uses the public IP to get the data back to your PC.

Because of NAT, you can have 200 devices in your house, but the internet only sees one address. Peak human engineering—or a clever hack to keep IPv4 alive!

Port Forwarding

If you’re new, port forwarding sounds scary. If you’ve been doing DevOps for 10 years, it still scares you.

Here’s the deal: your router has 1 public IP, but many devices inside with private IPs. When traffic comes to publicIP:port, the router doesn’t know which device to send it to. Port forwarding fixes that: you tell the router, “if anyone comes to port 443, send it to my PC at 192.168.1.10:443.”

Fun Facts

  • Encrypted data is like shouting an inside joke on the street—everyone sees it going somewhere, but only the friend understands.
  • The router keeps a NAT table to track connections, making sure responses go to the right device—like customs checking packages.
